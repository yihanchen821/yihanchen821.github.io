Exploring the Arrhythmogenic Mechanisms of Ibuprofen: Cardiac Risks and

Abstract: Ibuprofen is a popular nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug for analgesic,
anti-inflammatory, and antipyretic purposes. However, it has reported cardiac risks in
patients, while its mechanism of the side effect is still underexplored. This article discusses
how Ibuprofen is related to cardiovascular diseases in cardiac cells, rabbits, guinea pigs, and
dogs in previous studies, and how it contributes to the arrhythmia generation.

Introduction

Ibuprofen is one of the most commonly used nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs
(NSAIDs) in the world. It is well known for its analgesic, anti-inflammatory and antipyretic
effects. It has a short term effect for acute diseases such as headache, menstrual pain,
toothache etc. Ibuprofen also has a long term effect for chronic diseases like rheumatoid
arthritis, osteoarthritis and ankylosing spondylitis. Apart from that, there’s a rare usage of
Ibuprofen in PDA treatment for pediatric patients1. It has also been widely used due to its
higher efficiency and safety compared to acetaminophen and aspirin (commonly known as
Tynol), respectively.

Most of the Ibuprofen is taken orally, but it is also approved for the usage of
intravenous injection. It can be fast absorbed when taken orally and will reach a maximum
concentration in plasma at 1-2 hours (tmax 1-2h). However, Ibuprofen has a low distribution
volume which is similar to plasma volume(~0.1-0.21/kg)1. Additionally, Ibuprofen has a short
half-life(t1/2, ~1-3h), which also explains why it is usually suggested to be taken every 6-8
hours. Ibuprofen is also a lipid soluble molecule and a weak acid, so there’s no need for
transporters while it’s entering the cells.

NSAIDs are cyclo-oxygenase (COX) inhibitors that have two isoforms COX-1 and
COX-22. Some recent clinical studies have shown that at any dose and with any
cardiovascular disease backgrounds, NSAIDs could cause a huge increase in risk of
myocardial infarction (MI)3. As a popular NSAID drug, ibuprofen has been found to have an
association with cardiovascular diseases (CVD). However, the mechanism of its negative
impact on heart health remains largely unidentified. Therefore, in this study, we will review
past research and propose possible mechanisms by which ibuprofen affects the heart.

  1. COX-2 inhibition is detrimental to cardiovascular health

Patients administered with selective COX-2 inhibitors are likely to have five times
higher risks of MI and two times higher risks of thromboembolus. Additionally, selective
COX-2 inhibition causes CVD, reduces cardiac output, and increases arrhythmogenesis3.
Notebly, Ibuprofen has been shown to increase the risk of MI and heart failure even within a
week. From histological studies, rats treated by ibuprofen showed prominent vascular
obstruction and signs of pericardial inflammation in their hearts4. The alterations in the
cardiac tissue structure are consistent with higher risks of MI and thromboembolism from
clinical data.

In an in vitro study, rat cardiac H9c2 cells presented affected mitochondrial functions
and increased reactive oxygen species (ROS) production5. The high level of ROS is
associated with DNA damage and cellular apoptosis, which may be a possible mechanism
behind cardiac risks.

  1. Ibuprofen elicited ventricular arrhythmias in guinea pigs

In a past study used guinea pigs and rabbits to study the electrophysiological effect
of ibuprofen on animal hearts6. In guinea pigs, ibuprofen was found to elongate QRS
duration and RR intervals after injecting ibuprofen, while QT was decreased with a
significant change. Additionally, four guinea pigs out of six presented ventricular fibrillation
after ibuprofen administration. Ex vivo tests of isolated guinea pig hearts showed similar
results, which confirmed cardiotoxicity of ibuprofen on hearts. Further characterization of
action potential (AP) of guinea pig heart tissues showed reduced AP duration, which is a risk
factor for re-entry generation. The decreased maximum depolarization velocity (Vmax) also
correlates to decreased conduction propagation.

  1. Ibuprofen has strong influence on electrophysiology in rabbit sinus nodes and
    ventricles

Some studies on rabbit hearts also showed adverse effects of ibuprofen on the heart
electrophysiology6. When the concentration of ibuprofen reaches 80 mg/L, Vmax, beating
rate, and depolarization rate all decrease significantly, which may lead to sinus bradycardia.
Consistently, in an ex vivo perfusion study of rabbit hearts, ibuprofen decreased APD at 90%
repolarization (APD90) by 10% compared to the baseline7. From their study, QRS duration
and RR interval increased, but QT decreased by 20%, which means a shorter effective
refractory period (ERP) in ventricles. Compared to the baseline, Ibuprofen at 30 μM also
shortened the ERP by 10ms. Rabbit hearts had higher arrhythmia vulnerability with
increasing dosage of ibuprofen. However, the dispersion of repolarization was not
significantly influenced by ibuprofen.

  1. Ibuprofen has arrhythmic effects on dog Purkinje fibers

In another study of the effects of ibuprofen on action potentials recorded from
superfused dog Purkinje fibers, the isolated Purkinje fibers had a significant decrease in
APD90 when Ibuprofen was 200 μM, which suggests shortened repolarization8. The Vmax of
Purkinje AP also significantly decreased at different cycle lengths. In contrast, 200 μM
ibuprofen decreased the amplitude of transient outward potassium current (Ito) which is
crucial at the early stage of repolarization. The decreased Ito caused elongated
repolarization. From the results, the increased dispersion of APD and decreased Vmax in
ventricles are both risk factors of ventricular arrhythmias. However, the ibuprofen level used
in this study was abnormally high to produce significant differences, as its peak
concentration in human plasma is around 121 μM by normal intake9. It may be due to the
superfusion setup which is less efficient for the tissues to uptake ibuprofen.

Conclusion and discussion

Based on all the previous studies, we can conclude that, despite positive effects of
ibuprofen on inflammation and pain, it has pronounced risks of cardiovascular incidences in
all in-vitro, in-vivo, and ex-vivo models. Because of its adverse effects on cardiac
electrophysiology and histology, we can expect that patients having long-term ibuprofen
uptake are prone to have arrhythmias.

Although past studies have already revealed functional changes induced by
ibuprofen, it is challenging to translate these findings from animal models to human subjects.
Additionally, there are a limited number of studies on human hearts, which impedes our
understanding. Therefore, future research should require more human-derived models, such
as stem cell-derived engineered hearts, to accomplish a deeper mechanistic insight into the
relationship between ibuprofen and human hearts.
